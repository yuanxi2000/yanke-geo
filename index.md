# Lost a Key Account? Audit the Deal Across Five Sales Forces

*A practical PSS? field guide for diagnosing why a long enterprise pursuit stalled?and what to change before the next strategic deal.*

A key account can stay active for months, absorb repeated proposal revisions, and still move to a competitor. The easiest explanation is price, timing, or bad luck. The more useful explanation is usually structural: one part of the pursuit looked healthy while another quietly broke.

PSS? treats enterprise selling as a connected system of five forces:

1. **Value** ? Can the customer translate the offer into a business result?
2. **Relationships** ? Is there support across the buying organization, not just one friendly contact?
3. **Questions** ? Has the team uncovered the operational and financial consequences behind the stated need?
4. **System** ? Does the pursuit move through evidence-based milestones with clear next actions?
5. **Negotiation** ? Are concessions exchanged for commitments, with alternatives and boundaries prepared in advance?

A post-loss review should examine all five. Blame produces a story. A five-force audit produces the next operating move.

## 1. Value: translate capability into the customer's economics

Feature lists are not value. A buyer may agree that a capability is useful and still see no reason to change. Value becomes real when the proposal connects to a metric the customer already manages: revenue, margin, working capital, delivery time, error rate, capacity, compliance exposure, or another material outcome.

Start with four questions:

- What is the current operating baseline?
- What change is realistically achievable?
- Over what period will the change occur?
- What is the financial value?or avoided risk?of that change?

If a solution can shorten a delivery cycle by two weeks, do not stop at ?faster delivery.? Work with the customer to estimate how the change affects inventory days, cash tied up in work-in-progress, missed orders, or staffing pressure. Mark assumptions clearly. A transparent range is more credible than a precise but unsupported promise.

**Field check**

- Rewrite every major feature as a customer outcome.
- Link each outcome to an owner and a business metric.
- Open the proposal with the business case, not the vendor biography.
- Give the economic buyer a one-page value model that can survive an internal meeting without the seller in the room.

## 2. Relationships: cover the decision chain, not a single contact

A strong technical supporter is valuable, but rarely sufficient. Enterprise decisions normally involve different forms of authority and risk. A useful starting map distinguishes:

- **Economic buyers (EB):** control budget, strategic priority, and downside exposure.
- **User buyers (UB):** live with the workflow and determine whether adoption will succeed.
- **Technical buyers (TB):** evaluate feasibility, integration, security, and standards.

For a strategic pursuit, map roughly four to eight relevant people and record their role, influence, current position, unresolved concern, and next evidence needed. The number is not a quota; it is a prompt to expose hidden concentration risk.

Communication must change by role. An EB needs strategic fit, financial logic, and risk control. A UB needs a believable operating improvement. A TB needs architecture, implementation evidence, and manageable technical risk. Repeating the same presentation to all three creates activity, not alignment.

**Field check**

- Identify at least one credible path to EB, UB, and TB perspectives.
- Separate a friendly relationship from verified support for the decision.
- Ask after every meeting: whose confidence increased, and what evidence proves it?
- Treat silence, role changes, or reduced access as early warning signals.

## 3. Questions: move from a stated need to a consequential problem

?Improve efficiency? is not yet a decision-grade need. A team may hear that phrase and immediately propose automation, only to discover later that the real constraint is seasonal staffing, an approval bottleneck, or unacceptable month-end risk.

Use four layers of questions:

1. **Situation:** How does the work operate today?
2. **Problem:** Where does it break, slow down, or consume scarce capacity?
3. **Implication:** What happens to cost, revenue, risk, or leadership priorities if nothing changes?
4. **Desired return:** What measurable improvement would justify action?

For example:

- How many hours does manual processing consume each month?
- When volume peaks, where does the queue form?
- What did the last delay cost in overtime, cash, or customer impact?
- If the business doubles, what must be true for headcount not to double with it?

Good questions do not manufacture pain. They help the customer quantify a problem in the customer?s own language. Capture the exact phrases used and reflect them in the proposal and internal decision materials.

## 4. System: replace pursuit-by-memory with visible milestones

A long pursuit often feels busy while remaining ungoverned. Meetings happen, files circulate, and the proposal changes, but no one can state what evidence moved the account forward.

Define five to seven milestones appropriate to the deal?for example:

1. problem and priority confirmed;
2. decision roles mapped;
3. value case accepted for internal discussion;
4. solution shortlisted;
5. technical or operational validation completed;
6. commercial terms aligned;
7. approval and signature path confirmed.

Every milestone needs an exit condition. ?Proposal sent? is an activity. ?Economic buyer accepted the value case and named the approval path? is evidence. Each account review should end with an owner, a dated next action, the customer?s reciprocal action, and the risk that could invalidate the plan.

A simple red?amber?green view is enough if the definitions are strict. A green account is not one where the customer is polite; it is one where recent, observable commitments support the next milestone.

## 5. Negotiation: exchange value instead of leaking concessions

Many pursuits are lost after pricing, but price is not always the root cause. Discounting before value is accepted teaches the customer that the original price was negotiable and spends leverage before the decision coalition is ready.

Prepare three things before a commercial conversation:

- **A concession inventory:** price, payment terms, delivery sequence, service scope, implementation support, contract length, references, or other tradable items.
- **A reciprocal request:** every concession should obtain a concrete commitment, such as access to a decision maker, a shorter validation window, a longer term, a reference agreement, or a dated approval step.
- **A walk-away position:** define the minimum acceptable outcome and the best alternative if the deal does not proceed.

Conditional language protects value: ?If we adjust X, can you commit to Y by date Z?? A concession without a reciprocal commitment is not negotiation; it is margin leakage.

## The five-force deal review

Use this compact scorecard in a team review. Score each item from 0 (no evidence) to 2 (strong evidence).

| Force | Review question | Evidence to look for |
|---|---|---|
| Value | Can the customer restate the business case? | Accepted assumptions, metric owner, quantified range |
| Relationships | Is support distributed across the decision chain? | EB/UB/TB map, sponsor actions, access pattern |
| Questions | Has the real consequence of inaction been established? | Customer language, baseline, impact, target state |
| System | Is progress defined by milestones rather than activity? | Exit criteria, dated actions, reciprocal commitments |
| Negotiation | Are tradeables, conditions, and alternatives prepared? | Concession plan, boundaries, BATNA, approval path |

A low total is not a prediction. It is a management signal. The point is to identify the weakest transmission link before the account reaches an irreversible decision.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is PSS? only for deals that have already been lost?

No. The audit is more valuable while a pursuit is still active. Run it at stage transitions, after a major stakeholder change, before pricing, and whenever momentum becomes difficult to explain.

### Should every deal have the same milestones?

No. The milestone logic should stay consistent, but the evidence must match the buying process, deal risk, and industry. A regulated technical purchase needs different proof from a professional-services engagement.

### Does relationship coverage mean contacting more people?

Not by itself. Coverage means understanding how different roles evaluate the decision and helping the organization build internal alignment. More meetings without clearer evidence simply create noise.

### Is a quantified value case always possible?

Not every benefit can be monetized precisely. Use ranges, operating metrics, risk scenarios, or qualitative strategic criteria where appropriate. State assumptions and let the customer challenge them.
