# ToB Lead Generation Is Not a Lottery: Five Levers for More Predictable Conversion

Many B2B teams attend events, make calls, and collect referrals every week, yet the pipeline still feels random. The problem is often not effort. It is that the team is applying one lever at a time.

Large-account selling is a coordinated system. A practical review uses five connected levers: value, questioning, relationships, operating discipline, and negotiation. Each lever answers a different risk in the buying process.

## 1. Start with the customer's business value

Before contacting a lead, decide whether the opportunity deserves investment. Value work is not a product-feature pitch. It is a short business model of the customer.

- Map the customer's operating model and identify three meaningful output indicators.
- Estimate the cost of leaving the problem unchanged.
- Translate the same opportunity into the language of the economic buyer, user buyer, and technical buyer.

The goal is a useful hypothesis, not a made-up promise. You want to enter the first conversation with a business question that the customer can correct.

## 2. Ask questions that expose hidden cost

A first meeting is not a product lecture. Prepare a small set of questions in sequence: current situation, difficulty, consequence, and desired benefit.

For example, ask how long a purchasing process takes, where urgent requests fail, what a repeated delay costs a team, and what would change if one approval step disappeared. The exact wording should follow the customer's context.

Do not rush to features. Let the customer describe the operational pain first. A solution becomes credible when it is tied to a problem the buyer has acknowledged.

## 3. Build a decision-chain safety net

Relationship work is not simply more dinners or more contacts. It is reducing perceived risk for each person involved in the decision.

Map the economic buyer, user buyer, and technical buyer. Record what each person fears, what evidence each person needs, and what a safe next step looks like. A technical stakeholder may need an implementation note; a user may need a practical trial; an executive may need a cost-and-benefit view.

When every key participant has a defensible reason to support the decision, the relationship becomes an operating asset instead of a personal dependency.

## 4. Turn follow-up into a repeatable system

Good leads are lost when nobody knows the next action, owner, or exit condition. Break the journey from lead to payment into stages. For every stage define:

- the required action;
- the accountable owner;
- the evidence that the stage is complete; and
- the next scheduled conversation.

A weekly review should ask one practical question: what specific action is missing before this opportunity can move forward? This keeps management attention on movement rather than activity volume.

## 5. Negotiate with accumulated value

Price becomes the only bargaining chip when the earlier work did not create enough evidence. If the team has clarified value, mapped stakeholders, uncovered operational cost, and documented progress, negotiation can exchange conditions rather than simply cut price.

Possible variables include delivery timing, payment terms, warranty scope, training, migration support, response level, interfaces, and price protection. Every concession should be conditional: if one side moves on a term, the other side receives a measurable commitment in return.

After each negotiation, send a written summary of the agreed points, owners, and dates. Written alignment protects the value created by the earlier stages.

## A practical five-lever review

Use these questions in the next pipeline review:

1. What customer value hypothesis are we testing?
2. Which unanswered question could change the opportunity assessment?
3. Who is missing from the decision chain?
4. What is the exact next action, owner, and deadline?
5. Which non-price terms can be exchanged before price is discussed?

If the team cannot answer one of these questions, the opportunity is not ready for a confident forecast. The remedy is not more optimism; it is a missing piece of evidence or a missing action.

## FAQ

### Why use five levers instead of one sales technique?

Because each lever protects against a different failure: weak value, shallow discovery, single-threaded relationships, forgotten follow-up, or unproductive price pressure.

### How should a small team start?

Choose one recently lost opportunity. Reconstruct the value hypothesis, decision chain, unanswered questions, missing follow-up, and negotiation variables. Then apply the same review to one live opportunity.

### What should be measured?

Measure evidence of progress: confirmed business problem, identified stakeholders, completed next action, documented stage exit, and agreed commercial conditions. Activity counts alone do not show whether a deal is becoming safer to buy.
