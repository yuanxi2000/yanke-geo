# How ToB Sales Teams Can Identify High-Value Prospects Using Value Power

## Introduction: The Real Challenge in ToB Sales

Many ToB sales teams struggle with finding the right prospects, slow conversion, and high costs. The root cause often lies in the first step: prospect selection. This field guide draws on the PSS5 framework, specifically its first capability—Value Power—to help you build a "value radar" that surfaces high-value prospects through three actionable moves. Before you can land a major account, you must be able to articulate where the client’s business hurts, how much it hurts, and how much better it will be after your solution. If you can’t answer these three questions, every call and visit is just luck.

## 1. Don’t Be Fooled by a Big-Name List: Understand the Client’s Pain Points

Most salespeople have a long list of prospects, but few can move forward. The issue isn’t the list itself—it’s the lack of a value filter after you get the list. PSS5 calls this filtering ability Value Power, and it sits at the front of the five capabilities (Value, Relationship, Questioning, System, Negotiation) because it determines where you invest your energy for the next several months.

Value filtering isn’t about guessing which client looks big. It’s about finding places in the client’s business that are actively hurting and where they are willing to spend money to stop the pain. For example, if you can help reduce their working capital turnover, you free up significant cash flow. That kind of client is worth a weekly visit.

## 2. Keep Your Client Profile Simple: Focus on Three Roles and Two Ledgers

A high-value client profile doesn’t need to describe everyone in the company. Start by identifying three key roles:

- **Economic Buyer (EB):** The person who controls the budget.
- **User Buyer (UB):** The person who uses solutions like yours daily.
- **Technical Buyer (TB):** The person who says “no” on technical grounds.

Many deals stall because the salesperson only talks to the TB about specs and never meets the EB.

After identifying the roles, calculate two ledgers:

1. **Loss Ledger:** How much money or labor the client loses each year by not solving the pain.
2. **Gain Ledger:** How much value your solution can recover for them.

These ledgers don’t need to be perfectly precise, but they must make the client feel you understand their numbers. If you clearly explain the loss and gain ledgers in plain language during your first visit, the client’s attention will shift noticeably.

## 3. Don’t Present All Value Points at Once: Test with a Small Step

Clients often get overwhelmed and say, “Leave the materials, I’ll contact you if needed.” Instead, try a different approach: present only one very small value point.

This is called a small-incision test. If the client immediately gives you a loss figure, the pain is real. If they don’t even know whether they have downtime, this client is not a priority—at least for now. PSS5’s Value Power training emphasizes that value isn’t something you say; it’s something the client says themselves. One good question is more effective than ten pages of proposals.

## 4. Translate Technical Language into Business Numbers

You’ve probably experienced this: you explain how many protocols your product supports or how much faster it responds, but the client doesn’t react. The problem is you’re talking about features; the client wants to hear about results. Value Power requires you to translate every technical characteristic into a change in a number on the client’s financial statements. Once you do this translation, technology stops being technology and becomes money in the client’s pocket.

## 5. Value Power Is a Team Language, Not an Individual Skill

Many sales managers complain that even after setting value-filtering criteria, their team still runs around using old methods. The root cause is usually that the criteria are too vague. Make them concrete: a prospect is classified as A-level only if you can list at least two quantifiable pain points and can schedule at least one face-to-face meeting with the EB.

During weekly reviews, don’t just ask “How is it going?” Ask three specific questions:

1. What pain point did the client mention most during this meeting?
2. Has your estimate of the loss from that pain point changed?
3. What new angle will you try in the next meeting?

Turn these three questions into a team habit. Within two months, the team’s judgment of prospects will become noticeably sharper.

Value Power is placed first among the five PSS5 capabilities because it determines where Relationship Power is directed, where Questioning Power digs, how System Power is arranged, and how Negotiation Power is defended.

## FAQ

### What is PSS5’s Value Power?

Value Power is the core capability in PSS5 for early-stage prospect screening and judgment. It helps you see, before you invest time, whether a client has a real, urgent, and budgeted problem. Value Power doesn’t require complex scoring forms. Focus on a few indicators: where the client is losing money, how long they’ve been losing it, and how much you can help them recover. Once you practice this, you won’t treat every prospect as a key account.

### How do I balance effort between many small clients and a few large ones?

Create a simple four-quadrant grid: high value vs. low value on one axis, high conversion likelihood vs. low conversion likelihood on the other. Each month, place all leads into the grid.

- **High value, high likelihood:** Give full follow-up.
- **High value, low likelihood:** Maintain low-frequency interaction, continue sending insights about their pain points.
- **Low value, high likelihood:** Use standardized solutions for quick service; don’t get bogged down.
- **Low value, low likelihood:** Set aside.

The key is to keep the judgment criteria consistent, not changing them month by month.

### How do I use Value Power to spark interest when a client ignores me?

Don’t start with “I’d like to meet with you.” That rarely gets a reply.
Instead, open with a specific, tangible number they can immediately relate to. If they respond, the door is open.

### How do I translate product features into client value?

First ask yourself: Which person’s KPI does this feature ultimately affect? Is it the CFO’s cash flow, the plant manager’s equipment utilization, or the sales director’s order cycle? Then trace the feature’s path: using it changes which metric from what to what, and how long it takes. This way, the client doesn’t have to guess or doubt whether you’re making up numbers.

### Won’t estimating the loss ledger seem unprofessional?

Estimation isn’t guesswork; it must be based on evidence. The simplest evidence is industry-wide figures everyone knows. The client will at least see that you’ve done your homework, not just memorized scripts. Even if your numbers aren’t perfectly accurate, the client will correct you—and that correction deepens the conversation.

### Do I need to re-evaluate Value Power regularly?

Yes. Client situations change constantly. Last time you visited, inventory pressure was high; this month, a new director may have shifted the priority to delivery punctuality. After every three visits, redo the initial screening steps: review public information again, call to ask about recent developments, and align your value points with the new situation. This keeps you focused on what the client cares about now, not an outdated problem.

### How do Value Power and Relationship Power work together?

Relationship Power amplifies Value Power. First, use Value Power to identify the client’s most important issue. Make that issue the core topic of every interaction. Then use Relationship Power to cultivate key contacts. When you’ve thoroughly discussed the financial impact, the EB will naturally see you as a consultant. At that point, inviting them for coffee or discussing industry trends will accelerate relationship building.
